Data Used in Statistics

A Quick Guide to Data Types and Measurement Levels (10-Minute Session)

Based on "Types of Data Used in Statistics" Document

1. The Two Fundamental Types

Qualitative Data (Categorical)

Used for descriptive analysis, focusing on non-numerical characteristics.

Definition:

Describes qualities or characteristics. It cannot be measured numerically but can be categorized. Often used to group data.

Key Feature:

  • No Arithmetic: Addition/subtraction is meaningless.
  • Categories: Examples include colors, gender, marital status.

Quantitative Data (Numerical)

Used for numerical analysis, focusing on measurable quantities or counts.

Definition:

Represents numerical measurements or counts. It can be measured and expressed numerically, allowing for arithmetic operations.

Sub-Types (Click to view):

  • Discrete: Counts (e.g., number of students, pages in a book).
  • Continuous: Can be any value in an interval (e.g., height, temperature, time).

Nominal Data (Labels)

Represents categories with **no inherent order or ranking**. Used for labeling variables without any quantitative value.

Characteristics:
  • Categories are mutually exclusive.
  • No numerical significance.
  • Cannot be ordered or ranked.
Examples:

Colors (Red, Blue), Gender (Male, Female), Marital Status (Single, Married).

Appropriate Statistics:

Frequency counts, percentages, and mode.

Ordinal Data (Ranked Order)

Represents categories with a **meaningful order or ranking**, but the intervals between the categories are not necessarily equal or known.

Characteristics:
  • Categories have a defined order or ranking.
  • Intervals between categories **may not be equal**.
  • Arithmetic operations are not meaningful (e.g., you can't subtract 'Good' from 'Excellent').
Examples:

Educational Level (High School, Bachelor's, Master's), Ranking in a Competition (1st, 2nd, 3rd), Likert Scale Responses (Agree, Neutral, Disagree).

Appropriate Statistics:

Median, percentiles, and non-parametric tests.

Interval Data (Equal Intervals, No True Zero)

Represents numerical values where the **intervals between values are equal and meaningful**, but there is **no true zero point** (the zero is arbitrary). Ratios are not meaningful.

Characteristics:
  • Equal intervals between values.
  • **No true zero point.**
  • Addition and subtraction are meaningful.
  • Multiplication and division are **not meaningful** (e.g., 20°C is not twice as hot as 10°C).
Examples:

Temperature in Celsius or Fahrenheit ($0^{\circ}C$ is not the absence of temperature), Calendar Dates (Year 2000, Year 2010), Scores on a Standardized Test (Score of 0 doesn't mean absence of knowledge).

Appropriate Statistics:

Mean, standard deviation, and parametric tests.

Ratio Data (True Zero, All Arithmetic)

Represents numerical values where the **intervals are equal and meaningful**, AND there is a **true zero point**. This means that **ratios are meaningful** (e.g., twice as much).

Characteristics:
  • Equal intervals between values.
  • **True zero point.**
  • All arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) are meaningful.
Examples:

Height, Weight, Income, Age, Time, Number of Units Sold ($0 means absence of the quantity$).

Appropriate Statistics:

Mean, standard deviation, coefficient of variation, and all parametric tests.
